Contributing Factors

Trauma in childhood from abuse or an unstable family environment may result in an individual developing a dissociative disorder. An individual may also get a dissociative disorder from any traumatic event throughout their life like a natural disaster or a war.

Biological Factors:

  • Genetic disposition to dissociative disorders - Although it is not hereditary it is possible for an individual to be more susceptible to the disorder if a parent has it.
  • Other mental disorders or disabilities - If an individual has other mental disorders or disabilities, it may become harder for them to cope with trauma, potentially leading to dissociation.
  • Age - The age an individual experiences a traumatic event could affect the way they deal with it.

Psychological Factors:

  • Coping skills - If an individual does not have adequate coping skills, they may become more vulnerable to a dissociative disorder. This is not always the case.
  • Beliefs - If an individual believes that to deal with trauma is to push it down, then it may lead to the development of a dissociation disorder.
  • Self-esteem - If an individual has a low self-esteem, they may find it difficult to believe that they need help and may instead pretend that they are okay.

  • Peers - If an individual is being further bullied by peers, they may be more likely to develop a dissociative disorder.
